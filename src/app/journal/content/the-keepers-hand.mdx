There is a moment in every reconstruction when the archives run out. The 1860
sheets gave us the tower to the last brick course; the photographs gave us the
proportions the drawings could not settle. But the walkway brackets — eight
cast-iron consoles carrying the gallery around the crown — defeated every
instrument we aimed at them. The shop drawing was inked too small, the
photographs too oblique, the automated tracers too credulous about
166-year-old linework.

## The mathematics pass

First, discipline. The crown had accumulated values from mixed sources — drawn
labels, scan measurements, derived ratios — each defensible, each slightly
inconsistent with its neighbors. We rebuilt it as one constrained system from
drawn anchors only: every joint on the brick module, every level from a labeled
dimension, the whole stack solved rather than assembled. The batter turned out
to be concave — the drawn profile steepens in its last ten feet — and the model
finally necked under its crown the way the photographs always insisted it
should. A standing doctrine came out of it: the eye verifies, the math tunes.

## The lens confesses

The lantern's optic had been carried as a placeholder extracted from an old
scene file — the one part of the lighthouse not born from code. Research
settled what actually stands behind the glass: the restored 1902 bivalve
Fresnel, relit in 2023 as the active aid to navigation. It is now built
procedurally — bullseye panels, catadioptric rings, brass and glass from
registry dimensions. The entire lighthouse now rebuilds from a bare clone with
one command. No binary secrets remain.

## The keeper's hand

The brackets required a new instrument entirely. We connected the modeling
session to a live viewport and discovered the annotation pen: a curve drawn by
hand over the model can be harvested as coordinates, faired against compass
geometry, and cast as iron. The bracket's S-curved arm went through the full
gauntlet — the drawn radii from the 1860 sheet, hyperbolic studies, spline
edits — and the shape that survived every comparison was drawn freehand in the
viewport, then faired to two tangent arcs that hold within inches of the
original ink. The final assembly — frame, sweeping arm, a half-heart at each
post, an acorn pendant at each tip — was ratified live, piece by piece, and
cast eight times onto the tower.

The last piece was hiding in plain sight in a photograph: the walkway carries a
fascia band around its full perimeter that conceals the brackets' upper
attachments. Only the pendants show below it. One octagonal band later, the
render and the photograph finally describe the same building.

The registry records the arm's provenance the way it records every dimension:
source, method, confidence. This entry reads differently from the others —
_drawn by hand, faired by compass_ — but it earns its place by the same rule
as the rest. When the documents fall silent, a trained eye and a steady line
are measurement instruments too.
