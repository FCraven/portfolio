Our lighthouse's crown looked almost right, which is the most dangerous kind of
wrong. Almost right survives casual inspection; it fails the moment someone who
loves the building looks at it for longer than a second. This week we retired
"almost" — with a wash-profile scan of an 1860 elevation, a section cut through
the panel band, and the oldest instrument in construction: counting.

## The frieze is plumb

The verdict that opened the week was an observation from the reference
photographs: the paneled band under the walkway stands dead vertical, while our
model let it lean with the tower's taper. The 1860 elevation settled it — we
scanned the drawing's wash shading row by row at its own drawn scale and plotted
the silhouette width against height. The batter runs right past the old tower's
top, dies at a documented break, a molded belt oversails it, and the frieze
above rises plumb to the deck. Our model now does precisely that, and the crown
finally sits on the shaft the way a capital sits on a column — because
structurally, that is what it is.

## Frames, not decoration

The recessed rectangles in that band had been modeled as applied trim: neat
stone borders laid over the brickwork. A horizontal section through the band on
the same 1860 sheet says otherwise. The frames are the wall itself — an outer
course flush with the field, a second course stepping inward, a third forming
the panel. The drawing shows the steps nested in plan; the measured widths
agree with the photographs to a tenth of a foot. So the trim came off and the
bond went in: the frames are now laid brick, in courses, in the same running
bond as the wall around them. The building has one construction system, and now
so does its twin.

## Eighteen courses, no opinions

Whitewash defeats photography — a century of paint fills every joint a camera
might count. So the layout was settled by arithmetic instead: the band stands
just under four feet; a brick course of the period is two and five-eighths
inches; the division lands on eighteen courses. The layout then tallies the way
a foreman would call it: three courses of field, one course of step, ten of
panel, one of step, three of field. Eighteen. The margins run in brick units
too — a course and a half beside each bracket arm. The model's grid now derives
from the course module rather than from anyone's taste, which is the point: a
dimension argued in feet is an opinion; a dimension counted in courses is a
fact.

## What the section knew

Two details came straight off the section cuts and into the build. A pair of
flanged iron portholes pierce the band's side faces into the watch room —
drawn in plan in 1860, confirmed low in a panel by a photograph, now cast twice
from one pattern like the rest of the crown ironwork. And the mortar: the real
joints are struck flush and read as bright lines, where ours sat recessed in
tidy shadows. The joints now stand at true three-eighths-inch scale, flush,
with the brick faces varying a few millimetres against them. Perfection, it
turns out, is the least realistic finish available.

## The four-face problem

One mystery dissolved into geometry. In photographs of the real tower you can
often see four of the octagon's eight faces; in our renders, never more than
three. The taper was suspected. The taper was innocent. An octagon's face
normals sit exactly forty-five degrees apart — and our entire camera ring had
been planted on compass points, which is to say, aligned dead-on with a face
normal at every station. Every camera we owned was standing in the one family
of positions from which a fourth face cannot be seen. The ring now sits
half a facet off, corner-on, and the model photographs like the building.

## Reproducible, verifiably

The week closed with an audit of a claim we like to make: that the entire
model rebuilds from source — scripts and a dimensions registry, every value
carrying its provenance — on any machine, any time. The audit found the usual
embarrassments (absolute paths with a home directory in them) and removed
them. The build now runs from a bare clone with one command, deterministic
down to the seeded jitter on each brick. One component remains on notice: the
Fresnel lens, the only geometry not yet born from code. Its rebuild is
scheduled, from first principles — a bullseye, its concentric prism rings, a
bronze cage. A lighthouse should not keep secrets from its own drawings.
